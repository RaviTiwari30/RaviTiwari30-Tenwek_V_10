  SPILACTONE FILM-COATED TAB 50mg (spironolactone/Systemic) vs DICLOACT PLUS TAB (diclofenac sodium/Systemic)

Interaction Effect

spironolactone has its effect reduced by diclofenac sodium

Severity Level

Moderate -

Documentation Level

Good -

Probable Mechanism

Coadministration of diuretics with nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)may reduce the antihypertensive and diuretic effects of diuretics. This may be due to impaired prostaglandin synthesis which may lead to salt and water retention. Studies have shown that concurrent use led to decreased natriuretic effects of diuretics. Additionally, NSAIDs also have nephrotoxic effects which may be exacerbated by diuretic therapy. The addition of an ACE inhibitor to this combination (NSAID + diuretic) will further increase the risk of renal impairment, especially in the elderly. Monitor concurrent use and increase the dose of diuretic if needed.

Actions to be taken

  1. Use combination with caution.
  2. Monitor renal function.
  3. Monitor serum potassium.
  4. Monitor blood pressure.

  DICLOACT PLUS TAB (diclofenac sodium/Systemic) vs ALPYRIN TAB 150mg (aspirin/Systemic)

Interaction Effect

diclofenac sodium causes additive toxicity with aspirin

Severity Level

Severe -

Documentation Level

Good -

Probable Mechanism

The concurrent use of salicylates (e.g. aspirin) and NSAIDs increases the risk of gastrointestinal bleeding. In addition, there is some evidence that NSAIDs may antagonise the antiplatelet effects and reduce the cardioprotective effects of low-dose aspirin. If the use of low-dose aspirin with NSAIDs cannot be avoided, the use of a proton pump inhibitor should be considered. Furthermore, concomitant use of aspirin may result in reduced levels and effects of several NSAIDs; this pharmacokinetic interaction has little clinical relevance as concurrent use of analgesic dose aspirin (or other salicylates) with NSAIDs is usually not recommended.

Actions to be taken

  1. Avoid combination.
